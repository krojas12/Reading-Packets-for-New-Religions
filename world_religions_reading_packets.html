Sikhism

"There is only One God, and all are equal before that God."

Founded
~1500 CE

Punjab Region (India/Pakistan)
Followers
~30 Million
Holy Book
Guru Granth Sahib
House of Worship

🗺️

Historical Origin & Geography

🏔️

The Punjab Region

Sikhism began in the Punjab — a fertile region in northwestern South Asia split today between India and Pakistan. "Punjab" means "Land of Five Rivers."

In the late 1400s, the Punjab was a crossroads of Hinduism (many gods, caste system) and Islam (one God). Many people were divided by religion, caste, and social class.

Into this world was born Guru Nanak, who taught a new message: there is only one God, all people are equal, and love, service, and honest living — not rigid rituals — are the path to God.

✨

The Origin Story

Around 1499, Guru Nanak went to bathe in a river and disappeared beneath the water for three days. When he emerged, he said: "There is no Hindu, there is no Muslim."

Sikhs believe Nanak was taken into God's presence and given a divine mission. He spent 25 years traveling across South Asia sharing his message of unity through devotional songs called shabads.

After Nanak, nine more Gurus (teachers) continued his work. The tenth, Guru Gobind Singh, declared that after him, the holy scripture itself would be the eternal Guru.

👤

Key Figures

🌟
Guru Nanak Dev Ji
1st Guru (1469–1539)
Founder of Sikhism. Taught that God is everywhere, all people are equal, and honest work combined with sharing is the path to God.
⚔️
Guru Gobind Singh Ji
10th Guru (1666–1708)
Founded the Khalsa in 1699 and established the "Five Ks." Declared the holy scripture the eternal living Guru.
📖
Guru Granth Sahib
The Eternal Guru
The holy scripture containing over 5,800 hymns. Sikhs treat it with the same reverence they would give a living Guru.
🌺
Guru Arjan Dev Ji
5th Guru (1563–1606)
Built the Golden Temple in Amritsar and compiled the Sikh scripture. Became the first Sikh martyr.
💡

Core Beliefs (The "B" of Belief)

1
Ik Onkar — One God: There is only one God (called Waheguru). He created everything, is everywhere, has no gender or physical form, and loves all people equally.
2
Equality of All People: Sikhism strongly rejects the caste system. Every person — regardless of gender, race, or religion — is equally valuable in God's eyes.
3
Three Pillars of Life: Nam Japna (meditating on God), Kirat Karni (honest work), and Vand Chakna (sharing with others) define how a Sikh should live every single day.
4
Karma and Reincarnation: The soul is reborn many times, shaped by one's actions (karma). The goal is to break free from the cycle of rebirth and unite with God — called Mukti.
5
No Idol Worship: Sikhs do not worship statues. Prayer and meditation focus on the inner spirit, not outward rituals or physical images of God.
🕊️

Practices (The "B" of Behavior)

🍽️
Langar (Free Kitchen)
Every Gurdwara runs a free kitchen open to all people regardless of religion. Volunteers cook and serve meals as an act of service and equality.
🎵
Kirtan (Sacred Music)
Devotional singing of hymns from the scripture. Music is central to Sikh worship, performed live with traditional instruments.
☬
The Five Ks
Initiated Sikhs wear five articles of faith: uncut hair, a comb, a steel bracelet, an undergarment, and a small ceremonial sword.
🙏
Nitnem (Daily Prayer)
Devout Sikhs recite specific prayers three times a day — morning, evening, and night — as part of a daily spiritual routine.
🫂
Seva (Selfless Service)
Volunteering and serving others without pay or recognition. Sikhs believe serving others is the same as serving God directly.
📖
Ardas (Community Prayer)
A formal standing prayer at the Gurdwara or at home, beginning by remembering the Ten Gurus and asking blessings for all humanity.
🤝

Community & Belonging (The "B" of Belonging)

🏛️ The Gurdwara
Open to everyone — Sikh or not. Visitors remove shoes and cover their heads. The Gurdwara is the center of Sikh community life.
🌍 The Sangat
The congregation of Sikhs who gather to pray, serve, and support one another — a spiritual family open to all.
💛 The Khalsa
Initiated Sikhs join a global community of equals. All men use "Singh" (Lion) and women "Kaur" (Princess) as surnames — a powerful shared identity.
🎉 Gurpurabs (Festivals)
Festivals celebrating the Gurus' birthdays, with processions, music, and community meals that bring Sikhs together in joy and remembrance.
🌐 Global Community
Large Sikh communities in India, the UK, Canada, and the US feel connected through shared identity, langar, and shared values of equality and service.
📚

"Live and let live. Harm no living being."

🗺️

Historical Origin & Geography

🏛️

The Ganges Plain, India

Jainism originated in northeastern India, in the ancient kingdoms along the Ganges River — present-day Bihar and Uttar Pradesh. Buddhism also arose here around the same period.

Jainism is one of the oldest religions in the world. Around 600 BCE, Mahavira revived and organized its teachings at a time when Indian society was dominated by Hindu rituals and social inequality. Mahavira rejected this, teaching that any person could achieve liberation through nonviolence, truthfulness, and self-discipline.

✨

The Origin Story

Vardhamana was born around 599 BCE into a noble family in Bihar, India. At age 30, he gave up all his wealth, left his family, and became a wandering monk with almost nothing.

For 12 years, he practiced extreme discipline — fasting, remaining completely still during meditation, moving carefully to avoid harming even tiny insects. At the end of 12 years, he achieved Kevala Jnana — perfect, unlimited knowledge.

He became known as Mahavira ("Great Hero") and Jina ("Conqueror") — one who conquers inner passions like anger, greed, and pride. His followers became known as Jains.

👤

Key Figures

🌟
Mahavira
24th Tirthankara (~599–527 BCE)
Revived and organized Jainism. After 12 years of intense meditation, he achieved perfect knowledge and taught the path to liberation for all beings.
🏔️
Rishabhanatha
1st Tirthankara
Considered the first Tirthankara of the current cosmic cycle — Jains believe he lived millions of years ago and was the first to teach Jain principles.
📖
The 24 Tirthankaras
Enlightened Teachers
"Ford-makers" — 24 enlightened beings who appear in each cosmic cycle to guide souls toward liberation. Revered but not worshipped as gods.
🕊️
Parshvanatha
23rd Tirthankara (~900 BCE)
The Tirthankara before Mahavira, whose teachings Mahavira built upon — evidence that Jainism predates the 6th century BCE.
💡

Core Beliefs (The "B" of Belief)

1
Ahimsa — Nonviolence Above All: Every living being — from humans to animals to tiny insects — has a soul and deserves to live without harm. Jains try to avoid hurting any creature, even accidentally. Most Jains are strict vegetarians.
2
No Creator God: Jainism does not believe in a God who created the universe. Instead, Jains honor the Tirthankaras — fully enlightened beings — as the highest examples of what the soul can achieve.
3
Karma as a Physical Force: In Jainism, karma is an actual invisible substance that sticks to the soul because of actions and thoughts. It keeps the soul trapped in the cycle of rebirth. Liberation means completely shedding all karma.
4
Anekantavada — Many Perspectives: No single viewpoint can capture the whole truth. This principle encourages humility and openness — making Jains historically very tolerant of other religions and beliefs.
5
Liberation Through Self-Discipline: Through rigorous discipline, meditation, and detachment from worldly pleasures, the soul sheds all karma and achieves Moksha — perfect peace and freedom from rebirth forever.
🧘

Practices (The "B" of Behavior)

🥗
Strict Vegetarianism
Jains avoid all meat, fish, and eggs. Many also avoid root vegetables like onions, since pulling them harms micro-organisms in the soil.
🙏
Samayika (Meditation)
A daily 48-minute period of complete stillness and reflection. During this time, a Jain meditates with total detachment from all worldly concerns.
🌗
Paryushana Fasting
An 8–10 day annual festival of fasting and seeking forgiveness from everyone one may have hurt — concluding with "Micchami Dukkadam" (universal forgiveness).
🤲
Charity (Daan)
Giving to monks, nuns, and the poor. Jains are known for running hospitals, animal shelters, and schools throughout India.
🪔
Temple Worship
Jains visit temples to honor the Tirthankaras through rituals with flowers and incense — a reminder of the soul's potential for perfect liberation.
🧘
Monastic Life
Some Jains become monks or nuns, taking vows of nonviolence, non-attachment, and strict austerity as the most direct path to liberation.
🤝

Community & Belonging (The "B" of Belonging)

🏛️ The Derasar (Temple)
Jain temples are gathering places for worship and festivals. Some of the most beautiful temples in the world — like Dilwara in Rajasthan — are Jain.
🫂 The Four-Fold Community
Jain society includes monks, nuns, laymen, and laywomen — all equally important and all supporting each other on the path to liberation.
🌗 Paryushana Festival
This annual festival of fasting and forgiveness brings the community together. "Micchami Dukkadam" means "If I have hurt you in any way, I ask your forgiveness."
🏥 Social Service
Jains run hospitals, animal clinics, schools, and charities. Community service is considered a spiritual practice that strengthens bonds within the community.
🌱 Shared Values
Nonviolence shapes what Jains eat, what careers they choose, and how they treat every living thing — creating a powerful shared identity wherever they live.
📚

Mahavira

Shinto

"The divine is present in nature, in ancestors, and in all of Japan."

Founded
Ancient (no set date)
Origin
Japan
Followers
~3–4 Million (active)
Sacred Texts
Kojiki, Nihon Shoki
House of Worship
Jinja (Shrine)
🗺️

Historical Origin & Geography

🗾

The Japanese Archipelago

Shinto is the indigenous religion of Japan — it developed naturally among the people of the Japanese islands thousands of years ago, completely unique to Japan and deeply tied to Japanese culture and identity.

Shinto has no single founder and no specific date of origin. It grew from the ancient Japanese belief that the natural world is filled with powerful divine spirits called kami. The word "Shinto" — meaning "way of the kami" — wasn't even used until Buddhism arrived in Japan in the 6th century CE, when the Japanese needed a name for their existing native beliefs.

✨

The Origin Story (Creation Myth)

According to the Kojiki (712 CE), in the beginning, heaven and earth were not yet separated — the universe was shapeless chaos. Lighter elements rose to form heaven; heavier ones sank to form earth. The first kami (divine beings) appeared in the heavens.

Two kami were given a special task: Izanagi (the male) and Izanami (the female) were ordered to create the land. They stirred the ocean with a heavenly spear, and when they pulled it out, the drops that fell created the islands of Japan.

Izanagi and Izanami then gave birth to countless kami — including Amaterasu, the sun goddess, who became the most important kami in Shinto. Japan's imperial family claims descent from Amaterasu, giving emperors both political and religious authority for centuries.

👤

Key Figures & Divine Beings

☀️
Amaterasu
Sun Goddess
The most important kami in Shinto. Goddess of the sun and the universe, enshrined at the Grand Shrine of Ise. Japan's imperial family claims descent from her.
🌊
Izanagi & Izanami
Creator Deities
The divine couple who created the Japanese islands and gave birth to many other kami. Their story forms the core of the Shinto creation myth.
⛈️
Susanoo
Storm God
God of storms and the sea, brother of Amaterasu — sometimes troublesome, other times heroic. Famous for slaying the eight-headed serpent Yamata no Orochi.
🌾
Inari
Kami of Rice & Foxes
One of the most widely worshipped kami, associated with rice, agriculture, and prosperity. Inari shrines (marked by fox statues) are the most numerous in all of Japan.
💡

Core Beliefs (The "B" of Belief)

1
Kami Are Everywhere: There are countless kami inhabiting the natural world — in mountains, rivers, trees, animals, and special objects. They can be major deities like Amaterasu, or as humble as the spirit of a beloved ancestor.
2
Nature is Sacred: The natural world is not just beautiful — it is holy. Shinto teaches that humans should live in harmony with nature, not dominate it. Many natural sites — like Mount Fuji — are considered sacred places of the kami.
3
Purity and Pollution (Kegare): Shinto places great importance on purity. Certain things create spiritual pollution called kegare. Purification rituals (harae) cleanse this pollution and restore harmony between humans and kami.
4
Humans Are Inherently Good: Unlike some religions that teach people are born sinful, Shinto teaches that humans are born pure and fundamentally good. Evil comes from outside forces — impurities or neglect of the kami.
5
Focus on This Life: Shinto has little theology about heaven or afterlife. It focuses on living well, maintaining harmony with the kami, honoring ancestors, and ensuring health and prosperity in this life.
🌸

Practices (The "B" of Behavior)

💧
Misogi (Purification)
Ritual washing of hands and mouth at a shrine's water basin before approaching the kami — cleansing body and soul before entering sacred space.
🏯
Shrine Visits
Visiting a jinja to pray and communicate with the kami. Japan has over 80,000 shrines. Worshippers bow twice, clap twice, bow once, and pray silently.
🏮
Matsuri (Festivals)
Local festivals honoring specific kami, with processions, traditional music, and carrying a portable shrine (mikoshi) through the streets.
📜
Omamori (Charms)
Protective charms from shrines carrying the kami's blessing — for safety, exam success, or protection from illness.
🏹
Harae (Purification Rites)
Ceremonies by Shinto priests using a wand of white paper strips waved over people or objects to cleanse spiritual impurities.
🌸
Ancestor Veneration
Honoring deceased family members at home shrines with offerings of food and prayer. Ancestors are believed to watch over and protect their living descendants.
🤝

Community & Belonging (The "B" of Belonging)

⛩️ The Ujiko System
Every village traditionally had a local shrine, and the people of that area — called ujiko — belonged to that shrine's community. A deeply local sense of belonging tied to place and land.
🏮 Matsuri & Community
Local festivals are major community events organized by shrine parishioners. The shared work of preparing and celebrating matsuri creates strong bonds between neighbors.
🎎 Life Rituals
Shinto marks major life events: babies blessed at shrines, couples married in shrine ceremonies, New Year welcomed at shrines — tying individuals to community and tradition.
🇯🇵 National Identity
Shinto is uniquely Japanese. Practicing Shinto connects people to the long history of Japan — its mythology, its imperial family, and its deep relationship with the land.
🏡 The Kamidana
Many Japanese homes have a small household shrine (kamidana) for daily offerings to the kami and ancestors — extending spiritual belonging into the family home itself.
📚

Key Vocabulary

Kami
Divine spirits or gods in Shinto, present in natural phenomena, places, ancestors, and special objects
Jinja
A Shinto shrine — the sacred space where kami reside and are worshipped
Torii
The iconic red gateway marking the entrance to a Shinto shrine, separating the sacred from the ordinary world
Matsuri
A Shinto festival honoring specific kami, often with processions, music, and community celebration
Kegare
Spiritual pollution or impurity that disrupts harmony with the kami; removed through purification rituals
Harae
Purification rituals used to cleanse spiritual pollution and restore harmony with the kami
Kojiki
The oldest written record of Japanese mythology (712 CE), recording Shinto creation stories
Kamidana
A small household altar or "god shelf" found in many Japanese homes for daily worship
Amaterasu
The sun goddess and most important kami; said to be the ancestor of Japan's imperial family
Omamori
Protective charms purchased at shrines that carry the blessing of the local kami
💡
Did You Know?Japan's most sacred Shinto shrine — the Grand Shrine of Ise — has been rebuilt on an adjacent site every 20 years for over 1,300 years. This ritual represents renewal and the passing of sacred craftsmanship from generation to generation. The next rebuilding will happen in 2033!

🗺️

Historical Origin & Geography

🕌

Persia (Modern-Day Iran)

The Bahá'í Faith began in Persia (now Iran) in the mid-19th century. Its founders were imprisoned and exiled, spreading the faith from Persia to Baghdad, Istanbul, Akka (Israel), and eventually the entire world.

In the 1840s, a young man known as the Báb announced he was a messenger from God — and that a greater messenger was soon to come. Muslim authorities saw this as deeply controversial. The Báb and thousands of followers were persecuted and killed. But the movement survived and transformed into the Bahá'í Faith under Bahá'u'lláh — today one of the most geographically widespread religions on earth.

✨

The Origin Story

On the night of May 22, 1844, in Shiraz, Persia, a 25-year-old merchant declared himself the Báb — "the Gate" — a messenger of God announcing a new age and a greater teacher yet to come. This date marks the beginning of the Bahá'í calendar.

The Báb was executed by firing squad in 1850. But the movement lived on. A Persian nobleman, Mírzá Husayn-'Alí, experienced a divine vision while imprisoned in Tehran in 1852. He later took the name Bahá'u'lláh — "Glory of God."

Bahá'u'lláh spent his life in exile — mostly in Akka, in present-day Israel — writing thousands of letters and books that became the foundation of the Bahá'í Faith. He died in 1892, but his writings guide a global community to this day.

👤

Key Figures

🌟
The Báb
Prophet-Herald (1819–1850)
Announced a new revelation in 1844 and foretold the coming of Bahá'u'lláh. Executed at age 30, his teachings ignited a global movement.
✦
Bahá'u'lláh
Founder (1817–1892)
The central figure of the Bahá'í Faith. Claimed to be God's messenger for this age and wrote extensively about the unity of humanity and the need for global justice.
🕊️
'Abdu'l-Bahá
Son & Interpreter (1844–1921)
Bahá'u'lláh's son and appointed interpreter of his writings. Traveled to Europe and America to spread the faith and lived as a living example of Bahá'í principles.
📖
Shoghi Effendi
Guardian (1897–1957)
'Abdu'l-Bahá's grandson, who led the global Bahá'í community for 36 years and translated Bahá'u'lláh's writings into English.
💡

Core Beliefs (The "B" of Belief)

1
One God, One Religion: Bahá'ís believe one God has sent many messengers — Abraham, Moses, Jesus, Muhammad, Buddha, Krishna, and Bahá'u'lláh — each bringing teachings for their time. All are part of one continuous, unfolding religion of God.
2
Unity of All Humanity: All human beings belong to one single race and one global family. Racism, nationalism, and prejudice are the most dangerous threats to human progress. "The earth is but one country, and mankind its citizens."
3
Equality of Men and Women: Bahá'u'lláh declared men and women completely equal — a radical idea in 19th-century Persia. Civilization can only advance through the full and equal participation of women in all areas of society.
4
Science and Religion Work Together: Religion and science are two wings of the same bird — both searching for truth. Any religious belief that contradicts established science should be reconsidered. God is the author of both the physical and the spiritual world.
5
World Peace and Global Unity: Bahá'ís believe humanity is destined to achieve world peace — and that building a just, unified world civilization is humanity's greatest task in this age.
🕊️

Practices (The "B" of Behavior)

🙏
Daily Prayer
Bahá'ís say one of three obligatory prayers each day. Prayer is a direct conversation with God — personal and sincere, with no priest or intermediary needed.
📖
Daily Reading
Bahá'ís are encouraged to read from Bahá'u'lláh's writings every morning and evening to nourish their spiritual life and stay connected to God's guidance.
🌙
Fasting (March)
Each March, Bahá'ís fast from sunrise to sunset for 19 days — a time of spiritual reflection, detachment from material habits, and deepened connection to God.
🏛️
Devotional Gatherings
Open to everyone, regardless of faith — prayers and writings from many world religions are read and sung together. Anyone from the community is welcome.
🌍
Community Building
Bahá'ís run children's classes, youth groups, and study circles open to all — part of a systematic effort to build communities based on unity and justice.
🗳️
No Clergy
The Bahá'í Faith has no priests. Communities are governed by democratically elected nine-member councils called "Local Spiritual Assemblies."
🤝

Community & Belonging (The "B" of Belonging)

🏛️ Houses of Worship
Ten Bahá'í Houses of Worship around the world — each a stunning 9-sided domed building open to all people of all faiths for prayer and meditation.
🌍 A Truly Global Faith
With followers in nearly every country, Bahá'ís feel connected to a worldwide family that transcends nationality. Meeting a fellow Bahá'í anywhere creates an immediate bond of shared values.
📅 The 19-Day Feast
Every 19 days, communities gather combining prayer, community consultation, and social time. This regular rhythm builds strong, consistent bonds among members.
🗳️ Democratic Governance
Communities elect their own governing councils through a unique process with no campaigning. This creates a strong sense of shared ownership and belonging.
🧒 Children & Youth
Bahá'í children's classes and youth programs are open to all neighborhood children regardless of religion — creating intentionally diverse, inclusive communities.
📚

Zoroastrianism

"Good Thoughts, Good Words, Good Deeds."

Founded
~1500–600 BCE
Origin
Ancient Persia (Iran)
Followers
~100,000–200,000
Holy Text
Avesta
House of Worship
Fire Temple
🗺️

Historical Origin & Geography

🏜️

Ancient Persia (Modern-Day Iran)

Zoroastrianism originated in ancient Persia — the vast empire stretching across what is now Iran, Central Asia, and parts of Afghanistan. It was once the official religion of the most powerful empire in the world.

Zoroastrianism is one of the oldest religions still practiced today — and one of the most historically important. For over a thousand years it was the dominant religion of the Persian Empire. Its ideas about one God, angels, a devil, heaven and hell, resurrection, and a final judgment deeply influenced Judaism, Christianity, and Islam — the three religions you already studied in this class.

Today, Zoroastrians are a small community. Most live in India (where they are called Parsis, meaning "Persians") and in Iran, with growing communities in the United States, Canada, and the UK. Though their numbers are small, their influence on world history is enormous.

✨

The Origin Story

The religion was founded by a prophet named Zarathustra — called Zoroaster in Greek, which is how the religion got its English name. Scholars debate when he lived, with estimates ranging from 1500 BCE to 600 BCE. He most likely lived somewhere in the ancient steppes of Central Asia or Persia.

According to Zoroastrian tradition, Zarathustra had a powerful vision at age 30. While drawing water from a river, he saw a shining being of light who led him into the presence of Ahura Mazda ("the Wise Lord"), the one supreme God. Zarathustra was shown the true nature of the universe: a cosmic struggle between Asha (truth and righteousness) and Druj (lies and evil). Every human being must choose a side.

Zarathustra faced years of opposition from people who worshipped many gods. Eventually he won over a local king named Vishtaspa, and the religion began to spread. Centuries later, it became the official religion of the Achaemenid Persian Empire — the largest empire the ancient world had ever seen, stretching from Egypt to India.

👤

Key Figures

🌟
Zarathustra (Zoroaster)

After a divine vision of Ahura Mazda, he dedicated his life to teaching that there is one supreme God and that humans must choose between truth and lies in every action they take.
☀️
Ahura Mazda
The Supreme God
"The Wise Lord" — the one supreme, all-knowing, all-good God who created everything that is true and beautiful. Fire and light are his symbols.
👑
Cyrus the Great

The Zoroastrian founder of the Achaemenid Persian Empire. Famous for conquering Babylon and freeing the Jewish people from captivity — an act recorded in the Hebrew Bible.
😈
Angra Mainyu
The Destructive Spirit
The evil spirit who opposes Ahura Mazda — the source of all lies, suffering, and wrongdoing in the world. Zoroastrians believe good will ultimately defeat him at the end of time.

Core Beliefs (The "B" of Belief)

1
One Supreme God — Ahura Mazda: Zoroastrianism was one of the first religions in history to teach that there is only one supreme God. Ahura Mazda is all-knowing, all-good, and the creator of everything righteous. Evil has a separate source — the destructive spirit Angra Mainyu.
2
The Cosmic Battle Between Good and Evil: The universe is the scene of a great struggle between Asha (truth, righteousness, and order) and Druj (lies, evil, and chaos). Every human being must choose which side to support through their thoughts, words, and actions.
3
Free Will and Moral Responsibility: Zoroastrianism strongly emphasizes free will. Every person freely chooses between good and evil in every moment. God does not force anyone — the choice, and the responsibility, belong entirely to you.
4
Heaven, Hell, and Final Judgment: After death, every soul crosses the Chinvat Bridge (Bridge of Judgment). Those who chose righteousness cross safely to paradise with Ahura Mazda. Those who chose evil fall into darkness. At the end of time, evil will be destroyed and all souls purified.
5
Fire and Light as Sacred Symbols: Fire represents the light of Ahura Mazda, truth, and purity. Sacred fires burn continuously in Zoroastrian fire temples and have done so for thousands of years. Some fires in India are believed to have burned without interruption for over 1,000 years.

Practices (The "B" of Behavior)

🔥
Fire Temple Worship
Zoroastrians worship at fire temples where a sacred flame burns continuously. Worshippers pray facing the fire as a symbol of God's light and truth — they do not worship the fire itself.
🧣
Sudreh & Kusti
After a coming-of-age ceremony (Navjote), Zoroastrians wear a sacred white inner garment (sudreh) and a sacred cord (kusti) tied around the waist as symbols of faith and commitment.
🙏
Prayers (Gahs)
Devout Zoroastrians pray five times a day — once for each division of the day. Prayers are recited in Avestan, the ancient language of the scriptures, facing a source of light.
🌿
Respect for Nature
Zoroastrians are taught to protect earth, water, fire, and air — the four sacred elements Ahura Mazda created. Polluting these elements is considered spiritually wrong.
🕊️
Traditional Sky Burials
Traditionally, Zoroastrians placed the dead on circular stone towers (dakhmas or "Towers of Silence") to be consumed by birds — keeping the pure earth from being polluted.
🎉
Nowruz (New Year)
The Zoroastrian New Year, celebrated on the spring equinox (~March 21). One of the world's oldest holidays, now celebrated by over 300 million people across the Middle East and Central Asia.
🤝

Community & Belonging (The "B" of Belonging)

🔥 The Fire Temple
The Atash Behram (fire temple) is the spiritual and social center of Zoroastrian life. Communities gather here for prayers, festivals, and life ceremonies around the sacred flame.
🧣 The Navjote Ceremony
The initiation ceremony where a young person officially joins the Zoroastrian faith and receives their sudreh and kusti. A major community celebration marking their entry into the religious community.
🌏 The Parsi Community
Zoroastrians who fled to India over 1,000 years ago became known as Parsis. This tight-knit community — especially in Mumbai — maintains a powerful sense of shared history, heritage, and identity.
🎉 Nowruz Celebration
The spring New Year festival brings Zoroastrian families together for feasting and celebration. Though now widely shared across many cultures, it originated as a Zoroastrian holiday.
📚 Ancient Heritage
Being Zoroastrian means belonging to one of the oldest surviving religious traditions on Earth. The awareness of their ancient, world-shaping history creates a deep and proud identity — even within a small global community.

Key Vocabulary

Ahura Mazda
The supreme God of Zoroastrianism, meaning "the Wise Lord" — all-knowing, all-good, creator of all that is righteous
Zarathustra (Zoroaster)
The prophet and founder of Zoroastrianism, who received a divine vision of Ahura Mazda
Avesta
The sacred scriptures of Zoroastrianism, containing the hymns (Gathas) composed by Zarathustra himself
Asha
Truth, righteousness, and cosmic order — the force of good that Ahura Mazda represents and humans should embrace
Druj
Lies, evil, and chaos — the destructive force that humans must reject in favor of truth and righteousness
Angra Mainyu
The evil, destructive spirit who opposes Ahura Mazda — the source of all suffering and wrongdoing in the world
Sudreh & Kusti
The sacred white garment and cord worn by initiated Zoroastrians as symbols of their commitment to the faith
Navjote
The initiation ceremony welcoming a young person into the Zoroastrian faith community
Nowruz
The Zoroastrian New Year celebrated at the spring equinox; one of the world's oldest holidays
Parsi
Zoroastrians who fled Persia to India over 1,000 years ago; today the largest surviving Zoroastrian community

Did You Know?Many ideas that feel very familiar from Judaism, Christianity, and Islam — one God, angels, a devil, heaven and hell, resurrection of the dead, and a final day of judgment — appeared in Zoroastrianism first, centuries before these other religions developed. When Persian Emperor Cyrus the Great freed the Jewish people from Babylon in 539 BCE, Jews and Zoroastrians came into close contact — and scholars believe this is when many of these ideas were shared. Zoroastrianism may be the most influential religion most people have never heard of!
